Introduction

Greedy Piggies is a multiplayer card game made in Unreal Engine 5. It was published on Steam as the final project for this module. In this game, players use cards from a shared deck to compete. The core of each turn involves a bluffing and auditing mechanic. Between rounds, a shop system lets resources grow. This game did not just appear out of nowhere. Two main sources shaped its mechanics. Slay the Spire (Mega Crit, 2019) influenced the shop and resource loop. Poker provided the model for bluffing and holding players accountable.

This essay looks at these inspirations through academic frameworks. Doing this shows where the mechanics came from and what they actually argue. I will use Ian Bogost’s procedural rhetoric as the main tool (Bogost, 2007). Elizabeth Evans’ money games theory will help evaluate the game’s economy (Evans, 2016). Finally, Alenda Chang’s nature framework will look at the resource system (Chang, 2019). These design choices make claims about competition, fairness, and honesty. These ideas were taken from other games and then changed to fit Greedy Piggies.

Literature Review

Procedural Rhetoric — Bogost (2007)

Ian Bogost says procedural rhetoric is about persuading people through processes and computer code (Bogost, 2007, p.3). Games do not use dialogue to make points. Instead, they use what they allow or forbid. If a game punishes you for wasting resources, it does not need to say that planning is important. You simply feel the consequences. Bogost asks four questions about any game. What are the rules? Why do they matter? What do they say about the real world? How do players react? (Bogost, 2007). These questions help guide the analysis in this essay.

There is a big difference between telling and doing. A cutscene can say that lying is bad. A mechanic makes the results of lying feel personal and fast. This is why I am focusing on the auditing and shop mechanics in Greedy Piggies instead of the art style.

Money Games — Evans (2016) and Goldhaber (1997)

We need another framework to look at game economies. Michael Goldhaber says that in the digital world, human attention is the most limited resource (Goldhaber, 1997). Companies fight to keep your attention rather than just selling goods. Elizabeth Evans takes this further with the impatience economy. She looks at how free mobile games make money from people who hate waiting. In these games, play is mixed with long periods of doing nothing (Evans, 2016, p.10). These waits are built into the game on purpose. Players get frustrated and then pay money to skip the wait. Evans says this is all about making money from a player’s desire to keep moving (Evans, 2016, p.27). We have to ask where Greedy Piggies fits here. What does its lack of these fees say about fairness?

Nature in Games — Chang (2019)

Alenda Chang argues that video games act like ecological systems (Chang, 2019). They teach players to think about how resources flow and how people depend on each other. Chang usually looks at games with forests or oceans, but the idea works for any closed system. Greedy Piggies has no trees, but it has a closed ecology of chips and cards. These resources move between players and the shop. Using Chang’s lens shows that the card economy is a model of resource management. Scarcity is made by the rules, not by nature. The fact that the game has no natural environment is important. It means the system is entirely human made. This makes its arguments about advantage and fairness very deliberate.

Case Studies

Slay the Spire — The Shop as Procedural System and Ecological Model

Figure 2 - The cover art for Slay the Spire. (Slay the Spire, s.d.)

Slay the Spire (Mega Crit, 2019) was a major inspiration for the shop. Looking at it through these frameworks shows exactly what we borrowed. In that game, players spend gold on cards and relics. This system makes a procedural argument (Bogost, 2007). It rewards saving resources and claims that removing a weak card is just as good as adding a strong one. Efficiency is about cutting out the bad stuff.

Figure 3 - The shop in Slay the Spire. (Store - Slay the Spire, s.d.)

In an ecological sense, the economy is a closed loop (Chang, 2019). Gold comes from fighting and goes out through buying. The rules punish people who hoard money or spend it all too fast. This is systemic thinking. It shows that one spending choice affects the whole run. The shop in Greedy Piggies works the same way. Players who plan ahead get stronger. Those who spend randomly fall behind. This is not just a feature. It is a claim about the value of planning.

Poker — Bluffing as Procedural Argument

Poker ((Traditional). Standard ruleset.) gave Greedy Piggies its structure for hidden information. In poker, you cannot see what others have. You have to guess. The “call” mechanic is the heart of the game. Bogost would say poker does not just talk about deception; it makes players live it (Bogost, 2007). Every time you call a bet, you are acting out an argument about trust and risk.

Figure 4 - Professional Poker Game being played by myself

This was the model for the auditing mechanic in Greedy Piggies. When a player plays a hand, they claim it has a certain value. Anyone can trigger an audit to check if they are lying. The server then decides who wins or loses chips. This is a structural inheritance from poker. By using this digital audit, we imported the idea that lying is always an option, but getting caught is also a risk.

Greedy Piggies — Portfolio Case Study

Greedy Piggies combined these ideas into something new. This created arguments that are different from the original sources.

Using Bogost’s view, the auditing mechanic says something about trust. In this game, you have to verify everything. You can be dishonest by submitting a hand that does not match your claim. However, that choice has a cost. A good bluff helps you, but a good audit helps the challenger. The game does not lecture you about being fair. It makes fairness a choice you make every round (Bogost, 2007). Accountability is built into the rules.

The shop mechanic makes a different point. It suggests that having an early lead makes you even stronger later. Winners get more chips and better cards. This creates a loop where the rich get richer. But the auditing mechanic can break this loop. A smart audit can take chips from a leader. This gives a struggling player a chance to catch up. The game does not say if this makes things perfectly fair. It leaves that question open for the player (Bogost, 2007).

Figure 5 - The shop in Greedy Piggies.

Chang’s framework shows that the card economy is a closed world. Every chip someone gains is taken from someone else (Chang, 2019). This scarcity makes every choice matter. Players who think about the whole group will do better than those who play alone. The game teaches you to think about the environment of the game.

Finally, Evans’ theory shows what Greedy Piggies refuses to do. There are no timers or pay to win features (Evans, 2016). You buy the game once on Steam and that is it. All advantages come from playing. In a market where many games sell “skips” to frustrated players, this is a big statement. By not using these tricks, the game argues that you do not need to exploit players to be successful.

Conclusion

This essay looked at how the inspirations for Greedy Piggies can be explained with academic theories. These design choices were not neutral. They were arguments. By using ideas from Slay the Spire and Poker, we put specific claims into our own game.

Bogost shows that accountability is a part of the game’s structure (Bogost, 2007). Chang shows that the card economy works like an ecosystem where decisions affect everyone (Chang, 2019). Evans shows that by avoiding microtransactions, the game makes an ethical point (Evans, 2016). These inspirations are more than just borrowed ideas. They are arguments that become part of the code. The game remains honest by leaving the tension between lying and fairness for the player to solve.

Research Process

I started by asking which games influenced Greedy Piggies. I wanted to know how to explore those links using academic frameworks. Since Slay the Spire and Poker were already our main influences, the research went from the games to the theories. This made the analysis very specific. I looked at the mechanics first and then found the best frameworks to explain them.

I chose Bogost (2007) because it explains how mechanics carry meaning. The shop and audit systems are the most important parts of our design. I chose Evans (2016) because our game is on Steam. It was important to see if our economy was different from mobile games. Goldhaber (1997) helped support Evans’ ideas.

I almost did not use Chang (2019) because there is no nature in the game. But then I realized her ideas about resource flow fit perfectly with our card pool. This let me see the “ecological” logic of the chips and cards. It showed how players depend on each other in the game world.
